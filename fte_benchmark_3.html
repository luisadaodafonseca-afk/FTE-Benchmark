01 — Executive Summary

Workforce Efficiency Is Not the Risk — Under-Resourcing Is

This analysis benchmarks Exus's FTE efficiency against European peers across multiple metrics. The conclusion is unambiguous: the platform is operating at or near the efficiency frontier of the sector. The hiring plan under review represents the minimum staffing to sustain execution quality across the approved Business Plan.

Exus operates at 3–5× the workforce efficiency of comparable European peers — while managing the broadest capability set (9 out of 9 functions assessed) across 7 countries and 5+ technologies. The management team, which holds significant equity in the company, has no structural incentive to over-hire. The data in this analysis supports a clear conclusion: the 2026 recruitment plan of 39 FTEs is not an expansion request — it is the minimum resourcing to execute an approved €230–250M capital deployment programme without creating concentration risk.

Context for this Analysis

Over successive NCC meetings, questions have been raised regarding the pace and scale of Exus's hiring. This analysis was commissioned to provide the Board with an objective, externally benchmarked assessment of whether Exus's workforce model is appropriately sized for the platform it operates. The findings are clear: Exus manages more megawatts per employee, across more technologies and countries, at a lower personnel cost per MW, than any comparable platform in Europe. The 39 FTEs budgeted for 2026 represent the minimum staffing to execute the approved Business Plan. Delaying or reducing these hires does not materially reduce cost — it transfers execution risk from the P&L to the portfolio. Management, as a significant equity holder alongside PG, is structurally aligned with the objective of maintaining the leanest organisation consistent with responsible execution. This analysis provides the data basis for the Board and the NCC to reach a definitive conclusion on the adequacy of the current hiring plan.

07 — Conclusion

The Data Supports Full Approval of the 2026 Hiring Plan

Across every metric assessed — MW per FTE, cost per FTE, cost per MW, scope-adjusted efficiency, and forward trajectory — Exus operates at or near the efficiency frontier of its European peer group. The analysis leaves no reasonable basis for concern about over-hiring.

Exus manages 3–5× more capacity per employee than any integrated European peer. It does so across the broadest capability set in the benchmark — 9 out of 9 functions — while operating in 7 countries with 5+ technologies. The 39 FTEs in the 2026 recruitment plan represent the minimum staffing to sustain execution quality on a platform that is already operating at a level of efficiency no comparable company in Europe has achieved.

Summary of Key Findings

1. FTE Efficiency Leadership. Exus ranks #1 in the peer group at 25.0 MW/FTE (2026 Budget), exceeding even pure-play IPPs like Neoen (20.2) that operate narrower scopes. The peer median sits at ~6.5 MW/FTE.

2. Scope Exceeds All Peers. Exus is the only platform covering 9/9 capability areas (development, IPP, third-party AM, energy management, BESS, gas, data centres, industrial solutions, PPA structuring). The next broadest peer covers 5/9.

3. Improving Operating Leverage. The 2025→2026 trajectory shows capacity growing +43% while FTEs grow +22%. MW/FTE is improving, not degrading — the platform is generating scale economies.

4. Cost Discipline Intact. At €5,000 of personnel cost per MW managed, the workforce model is lean by any standard. Average cost per FTE of ~€126k reflects a high-quality, specialised workforce — but the aggregate cost base remains proportionate to a ~€2B enterprise value target.

5. Forward Trajectory Holds. Even at ~300 FTEs in 2030, Exus will maintain a MW/FTE ratio of ~22.6 — still more than 3× the peer median. The platform is scaling capacity faster than headcount.

Strategic Assessment

The 2026 recruitment plan of 39 FTEs represents the minimum staffing to execute the approved Business Plan across 7 countries and 6 business lines. Management, as a significant equity holder alongside PG, is structurally aligned with the objective of maintaining the leanest possible organisation. Every peer in this analysis operates with 3–8× more people per MW than Exus. The relevant question for the Board is not whether Exus is hiring too many people — no data point supports that concern — but whether the current staffing trajectory is sufficient to protect the portfolio that has already been built and to execute the capital programme that has already been approved.

No data point in this analysis — or in any comparable benchmark available in the European energy sector — supports a conclusion that Exus is over-staffed. The analysis supports management's request that the NCC approve the 2026 recruitment plan in full, and that future hiring within approved Business Plan parameters be treated as a management execution decision consistent with standard institutional governance for execution-led platforms.

1
Exus operates at the frontier of FTE efficiency in Europe.
At 25.0 MW per FTE (2026 Budget), Exus manages 3.8× the peer group median (~6.5 MW/FTE). Only Neoen achieves a comparable ratio (20.2), and Neoen does so with a materially narrower scope: 3 technologies, no third-party AM, no data centre development, no industrial solutions, no gas peakers. Larger integrated peers — Statkraft (3.3), Acciona Energía (4.8), ERG (4.4) — require 5–8× more FTEs per MW of managed capacity. The efficiency gap is structural, not coincidental.
2
Exus manages the broadest scope of any company in the benchmark.
Exus covers 9 out of 9 capability areas assessed: wind development, solar PV, BESS, gas peakers, data centre infrastructure, industrial solutions, third-party AM/O&M, energy management, and PPA structuring. No peer covers more than 5 out of 9. This means each Exus FTE is effectively responsible for a broader range of tasks, decisions, and regulatory interfaces than any comparable employee at a peer company. Scope-adjusted, Exus's efficiency advantage is even more pronounced.
3
The platform is generating operating leverage — capacity is growing faster than headcount.
Between 2025 and 2026, managed capacity increases by +43% (3.9 → 5.5 GW) while FTEs increase by only +22% (181 → 220). The MW/FTE ratio improves from 21.3 to 25.0. This is the opposite of organisational bloat — it is operating leverage compounding. The centralised GBS model, technology-first approach to asset management, and the inherent scalability of third-party AM services are all contributing to this dynamic.
4
The 2026 recruitment plan of 39 FTEs is the minimum, not an expansion.
Of the 39 new positions: 14 support existing projects that cannot be delayed without execution risk, 9 address minimum critical size requirements in country offices that currently operate below sustainable levels, and 16 are growth-related — enabling the pipeline expansion into UK&I and Germany that underpins the Business Plan. Delaying or cutting these hires does not save cost in any meaningful sense; it creates execution risk on a €230–250M capital deployment programme and jeopardises the platform's ability to deliver on its commitments to PG and the IC.
5
The 2030 trajectory confirms sustained efficiency at ~300 FTEs.
The Business Plan targets ~300 FTEs by 2030 managing ~6.8 GW of total capacity — a ratio of ~22.6 MW/FTE, which remains 3.5× the current peer median. The platform will by then span ~1 GW operational IPP, ~5 GW development pipeline, ~10 GW third-party services, and 3–4 Exus Digital projects. This is not an organisation that needs to be constrained further; it is an organisation that needs the minimum resourcing to deliver on one of the most differentiated platform strategies in European energy.

Proposed Actions for the Board & NCC

Based on the findings of this analysis, management proposes: (1) that the NCC approve the 2026 recruitment plan of 39 FTEs in full, consistent with the approved Business Plan; (2) that future hiring within approved Business Plan parameters be treated as a management execution decision, with the NCC retaining oversight of the compensation framework and aggregate headcount trajectory rather than individual role approvals; and (3) that the Board acknowledge the execution risk inherent in under-resourcing a platform at this stage of its scaling trajectory — a €2 billion enterprise value target supported by a €230–250M capital programme that requires adequate staffing to deliver. The prorated cost of the 39 new positions is €3.4M (€6.0M annualised) — a marginal investment relative to the programme it enables.

Methodology Notes

MW/FTE Calculation: Total managed capacity (operational + under construction + third-party assets under management, where applicable) divided by total company headcount. This is the most comprehensive and fair basis of comparison, as it captures the full scope of management responsibility per employee. For Exus, this includes both the internal portfolio (~1.7 GW in 2026) and third-party managed assets (~3.85 GW TPES in 2026 Budget). For peers, we use the broadest available capacity figure (operational + under construction + managed).

TPS Capacity Composition (Budget 2026 Slide 16): The 3,851 MW TPS portfolio is not pipeline — it is predominantly contracted management of operational assets. Breakdown by service type: Asset Management of operational assets: ~2,751 MW (71%); O&M of operational assets (existing + growth): ~255 MW (7%); Construction Supervision: ~681 MW (18%); Development Supervision: ~164 MW (4%). Total operational (AM + O&M): ~3,006 MW = 78% of TPS. This means the Op+Cons+TPS metric used throughout this analysis is overwhelmingly grounded in hard, revenue-generating, contracted capacity — not aspirational pipeline. The TPS column is functionally equivalent to "operational managed capacity" and should be treated as such for benchmarking purposes.

Headcount Definition: Total FTEs including all employees across all geographies and all functions (corporate, operations, development, G&A). Where peers report "average workforce" vs. "period-end headcount," we use the most recently reported figure. Contractor and outsourced labour is excluded for all companies.

Comparability Considerations: This benchmark deliberately compares Exus to companies with different business models to illustrate the breadth of Exus's scope. Pure IPPs (Neoen), vertically integrated developers (Acciona Energía, Grenergy), asset-heavy operators (ERG, Statkraft), and integrated platforms (BayWa r.e.) are all included. The scope-adjusted analysis in Section 04 addresses the apples-to-oranges concern by showing that Exus covers materially more capability areas per FTE than any peer.

VSB Group: Included as a directly relevant comparator — a pan-European renewable energy platform with a comparable business model to Exus. PG acquired 80% of VSB in 2020 (then ~300 employees, 1.1 GW developed, 1.4 GW managed) and sold to TotalEnergies in December 2024 for €1.57bn (then ~500 employees, 2.3 GW developed, 3 GW managed, 475 MW in operation/construction, 18 GW pipeline). FTE, capacity, and pipeline figures are from PG's own exit press release and TotalEnergies' acquisition announcement — high confidence. Personnel cost (~€48M, ~€95k/FTE) is estimated from German energy sector Personalaufwand benchmarks (Destatis) — medium confidence. On a hard capacity basis (Op+Cons+Managed), VSB's cost/MW of ~€16k is 3.2× Exus. On a pipeline-inclusive basis, VSB's ~€2.3k/MW appears more efficient than Exus's ~€3.1k/MW — but the 18 GW pipeline is predominantly early-stage land development requiring minimal management FTEs, whereas Exus's TPS portfolio is 78% operational asset management under contracted service agreements. The hard capacity metric is the more meaningful basis for workforce efficiency comparison.

Solaria DC Activities: Solaria has announced data centre ambitions. However, as of December 2024, these are nascent and not yet at the development stage that Exus Digital has reached (Setúbal grid bond posted, Bełchatów 100% owned, Hohe Börde under active development). Solaria's capability matrix reflects its current operational scope, not announced intentions.

Capital Energy: Included as a reference point only (not in the main ranking) because Capital Energy is primarily a development-stage company with limited operational capacity (~206 MW installed) relative to its pipeline (16 GW). Its ~390 FTEs against ~206 MW operational capacity would yield an extremely low MW/FTE of ~0.5, which is not comparable to platforms with significant operational or managed portfolios. However, it illustrates that development-heavy models are inherently more FTE-intensive per operational MW.
